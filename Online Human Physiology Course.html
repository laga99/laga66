3. Homeostatic Concept
The word homeostasis refers to the maintenance of overall endurance in the body. Homeostasis stabilizes the body by regulating the internal environment. It takes the body to function as it should be. The homeostatic process is essential for the survival of every cell, tissue, and body system [2]. Homeostasis in the general sense refers to stability, balance or equilibrium. Maintenance of a stable internal environment requires constant monitoring, especially by the brain and nervous system. The brain receives information from the body and responds appropriately through the release of various substances such as neurotransmitters, catecholamines, and hormones [2]. The physiology of individual organs is furthermore useful to facilitate the maintenance of homeostasis from the whole body eg Arrangement of blood pressure: the release of renin by the kidneys which allows the blood pressure to be stable (Renin, angiotensinogen protein, Aldosterone System), although the brain helps regulate blood pressure by pituitary hormones by releasing Anti-diuretic (ADH). 4.History
The study of human physiology at least back to 420 BC and also in the days of Hippocrates, the father of medicine [4]. Aristotle's critical thought and his emphasis on the relationship between structure and function marked the early journey of physiology in Ancient Greece, while Claudius Galenus (126-199 AD), known as Galen, was the first to use experiments to investigate bodily functions. Galen was the founder of experimental physiology [5] in the medical world moved from Galenism only to the appearances of Andreas Vesalius and William Harvey. [6]

During the Middle Ages, the ancient traditions of Greek and Indian medicine have been further developed by Muslim doctors. The important work in this period was done by Ibn Sina (980-1037), author of The Canon of Medicine, and Ibn al-Nafis (1213-1288), among others.

After the Middle Ages, the Renaissance brought about an increase in physiological research in the Western world that sparked the study of modern anatomy and physiology. Andreas Vesalius is the author of one of the most influential books on human anatomy, De humani corporis fabrica [7]. Vesalius is often referred to as the founder of modern human anatomy. [8] The anatomist William Harvey describes the circulatory system in the 17th century, [9] demonstrating a useful combination of close observation and careful experimentation to learn about bodily functions, which is fundamental to the development of experimental physiology. Herman Boerhaave is sometimes referred to as the father of physiology for being a Leiden teacher and a book because of his text, Institutiones medicae (1708).

In the 18th century, important works in this field were developed and researched by Pierre Cabanis, a French physician and physiologist.

In the 19th century, physiological knowledge began to develop at a rapid rate, in particular with the 1838 theories of Matthias Schleiden and Theodor Schwann.
